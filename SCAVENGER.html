    • The SCAVENGER project

    @SCAVENGERfellow

    Scavenging is found in systems ranging from deep sea vent systems to tropical rain forests, and in food webs accounts for more energy transfer per link than predation. Hence, in order to be able to predict and manage ecosystems and biodiversity in a changing world we must understand the drivers of scavenging behaviour. Despite this clear need to understand scavenging it remains a neglected topic with very little research focused on the evolutionary and ecological drivers of this foraging behaviour. While almost all predators engage in some level of scavenging, the extent to which species engage is hugely variable, both across species (vultures scavenge ~ 100% diet, weasels scavenge ~ 2% diet) and within species (different lion populations scavenge between a range 5% and 50%). One reason we donâ€™t understand this variation lies in the difficulty of observing and calculating the level of scavenging within species.

    This project aims to test a range of potential drivers of this variation in scavenging behaviour by using an agent based modelling framework that combines biomechanics, energetics and macroecology approaches. Using this agent based modelling approach, I will test the importance of scavenging in a range of groups, including fish, mammals, birds, reptiles, and across a range of systems, such as marine and terrestrial systems.
